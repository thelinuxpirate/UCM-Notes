** 8 Stages of Life
*** Prenatal Stage         (Begins at conception & ends at birth)
Most rapid period of growth
Divided into 3 periods:
- Germinal (first 2 weeks): cell division & implantation
- Embryonic(weeks 3–8): organ & system formation (things like nicoten affect it)
- Fetal(weeks 9–birth): growth & refinement of organs
*** Infancy & Toddlerhood  (0–2 Years)
- Pyhsical Development: Rapid brain development, sensory & motor skills
- Cognitive: Language development begins
- Psychosocial: Attachment to caregivers is critical
*** Early Childhood        (2-6 Years)
- Physical: Continued motor skill refinement (running, drawing).
- Cognitive: Preoperational thinking (Piaget) symbolic play, egocentrism.
- Psychosocial: Increasing independence, gender identity forms, peer relationships begin.

*** Middle Childhood       (6-12 Years)
- Physical: Steady growth
- Cognitive: Logical Thinking
- Psychosocial: Self-concept develops; friendships deepen; importance of school increases.
Puberty begins, & baby teeth fall out
*** Adolescence            (12-18 Years)
- Puberty: Period of rapid physical changes triggered by hormones (estrogen/testosterone).
- Brain Development: Prefrontal cortex (decision-making, impulse control) matures later.
- Sleep Shifts: Sleep later, wake up later
Adolescence is marked by both physical & cognitive maturation NOT emotional balence
Better planning, self-control, still struggle with impulsivity
*** Early Adulthood        (20s-40s)
- Physical: Peak physical condition, then gradual decline.
- Cognitive: Postformal thought may emerge; continued education/career development.
- Psychosocial: Intimacy vs. isolation (Erikson); focus on love, partnership, & career building.

Brain fully developed by ~= 25
Lifestyle choices impact your life
Reprodictive Peak
*** Middle Adulthood       (40s-60s)
- Physical: Noticeable aging signs; possible health issues.
- Cognitive: Stable abilities; expertise may grow.
- Psychosocial: Generativity vs. stagnation (Erikson); focus on parenting, mentoring, contribution to society.

Muscle mass decreases, metabolism slows down, vision/hearing decline
Menopause: End of menstration for women
Health risks increase
*** Late Adulthood         (60s-End of life)
- Physical: Slower processing, sensory decline, health conditions more common.
- Cognitive: Possible decline in memory or speed, but wisdom may increase.
- Psychosocial: Integrity vs. despair (Erikson); reflection on life, coping with loss & aging

- Noticeable physical decline: Loss of bone density, flexibility, sensory abilities.
- Brain shrinkage: Especially in the hippocampus & prefrontal cortex; processing speed slows.
- Neurodegenerative Disorders: Risk increases (e.g., Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s).
- "Successful Aging": Staying mentally, physically, & socially active is linked to better outcomes.

** Cognitive Development Theory - Jean Piaget
Idea: Children move through four stages of cognitive development.

- Sensorimotor (Birth-2)
- Preoperational (2-7, Children arent fully logical/mature)
- Concrete Operational (7-11)
- Formal Operational (12+)
*** Preoperational Thinking
- Symbolic Thinking:
  Children can use symbols such as words, images, & objects to represent things that are not physically present.
- Language Development:
  Rapid growth in vocabulary & language skills occurs as children use words to express their ideas & represent the world symbolically.
- Egocentrism:
  Children have difficulty seeing things from perspectives other than their own. They often assume others see, think, & feel exactly as they do.
- Animism:
  Children may believe that inanimate objects have feelings, thoughts, or intentions (e.g., thinking a teddy bear feels sad).
- Centration:
  Focus on one aspect of a situation while ignoring others, such as only considering the height of a liquid in a glass & not its width during conservation tasks.
- Lack of Conservation:
  Children do not yet understand that quantity remains the same even when the shape or appearance changes (e.g., believing a taller, thinner glass holds more liquid than a shorter, wider one).
- Lack of Reversibility:
  Difficulty understanding that actions can be undone or reversed (e.g., not realizing that a ball of clay rolled into a snake can be rolled back into a ball).
- Collective Monologues:
  Conversations between children in which each talks without truly engaging with the other's perspective, reflecting egocentric thought.
*** Extensions to the Theory
Unofficial 5th Stage:
- Relativistic Thinking: Recognizes that knowledge & solutions are context-dependent, not absolute.
  Truth is understood as subjective & influenced by perspective.

** Stages of Sleep (REM)

- Stage 1: Light sleep, easy to wake, muscle activity slows.

- Occurs ~90 minutes into sleep.

** Why Do We Need Sleep?
- Cognitive Function: Enhances memory, problem-solving, & attention
- Evolutionary Theory: Sleeping at night kept us safe from predators
*** Restorative Theory
- Sleep allows the body to repair tissues, synthesize proteins, & replenish energy stores
- Slow-wave sleep (Stage 3 of NREM sleep) is particularly important for physical restoration
- REM sleep supports neural repair & brain plasticity
- Sleep deprivation impairs immune function, increases inflammation,
  & slows wound healing, underscoring the restorative role of sleep
*** Cognitive Function Theory
- Cognitive functions interact with sleep to keep your brain functioning optimally
- Sleep & cognitive functions are interconnected: sleep enhances cognition, & cognitive processes influence sleep patterns

Attention:
- Role in Sleep: Attention helps regulate sleep by signaling when you’re tired (e.g., noticing heavy eyelids)
- How It Works: When you’re sleep-deprived, attention falters, making it hard to focus (e.g., zoning out in class).

Memory:
- Role in Sleep: Sleep consolidates memories, moving information from short-term to long-term storage
- How It Works: During sleep, the hippocampus replays the day’s events, strengthening neural pathways

Problem-Solving & Decision-Making:
- Role in Sleep: The brain processes complex problems during sleep, often leading to creative insights
- How It Works: REM sleep (dreaming phase) enhances creative problem-solving by connecting ideas in new ways
*** Evolutionary Theory (Predatory Risk Hypothesis)
This hypothesis suggests that sleeping during darkness in safe locations reduced our ancestors’ exposure to predators, thereby increasing survival chances

Key Mechanisms
- Nocturnal Vulnerability: Darkness heightens predation risks, making inactivity (sleep) a strategic survival tactic.
- Safe Habitat Selection: Humans evolved to sleep in sheltered areas (e.g., caves, constructed shelters) to minimize danger.
- Energy Conservation: Reduced nighttime activity conserved energy while avoiding high-risk periods.

*** Activation-Synthesis Theory
Developed by neuroscientist Alan Hobson, this theory proposes that dreams are the brain’s attempt to synthesize
& make sense of random neural activity during REM sleep

Dreams are the brain’s attempt to make sense of random neural activity
Dreams are NOT necessarily meaningful or symbolic, but rather the result of the brain
trying to create a narrative from spontaneous brain activation

Dreaming may help construct a “virtual reality” in the mind, which could be useful

*** Neurocognitive Theory
Dreams reflect waking concerns & cognition—more continuity with real life
Dreams are shaped by the same neural networks that support imagination, memory, & daydreaming during waking life

Unlike older theories, neurocognitive theory emphasizes development: as children’s cognitive abilities grow, their dreams become more complex & story-like

** 3 Major Forms of Learning
*** Classical Conditioning - Pavlov
- Learning to associate two stimuli & anticipate events

Classical conditioning is a type of learning in which an organism comes to
associate two stimuli & anticipate events

Pavlov’s Discovery:
Pavlov noticed that dogs would salivate not only at food,
but also at cues associated with food—like the sound of a bell

Processes:
- Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS): Stimulus that naturally & automatically triggers a response
- Unconditioned Response (UCR): The unlearned, natural response to the UCS
- Neutral Stimulus (NS): A stimulus that doesn't elicit a response before conditioning
- Conditioned Stimulus (CS): Originally a NS that after the effects of UCS, triggers a CR
- Conditioned Response (CR): A learned response to a previously neutral (now conditioned) stimulus

- Acquisition: The initial learning of the stimulus-response relationship.
- Extinction: When the CS is presented repeatedly without the UCS, the CR weakens.
- Spontaneous Recovery: After a pause, a previously extinguished CR can return.
- Generalization: The tendency to respond similarly to stimuli that resemble the CS (e.g., dog salivates to different bell tones).
- Discrimination: Learning to respond only to the specific CS, not similar stimuli.

Examples:
- Phobias
- Taste Aversions
**** Phases of Classical Conditioning
- Before Conditioning:
UCS → UCR (Food → Salivation)
NS → No response (Bell → No salivation)

- During Conditioning:
NS + UCS → UCR (Bell + Food → Salivation)

- After Conditioning: CS → CR (Bell → Salivation)
*** Operant Conditioning - B.F. Skinner
A type of learning in which behavior is strengthened or weakened depending on its consequences
Unlike classical conditioning (which is passive), operant conditioning is active
the organism acts on the environment & learns from the results

Studied by: B.F. Skinner, which built upon Thorndike’s Law of Effect
**** Reinforcement & Punishment
- Positive Reinforcement: Add something desirable to increase behavior
- Negative Reinforcement: Remove something unpleasant to increase behavior

- Positive Punishment: Add something unpleasant to decrease behavior
- Negative Punishment: Remove something to decrease behavior

Reinforcement Schedules:
- Fixed Ratio:       Reinforcement after a set number of responses
- Variable Ratio:    Reinforcement after an unpredictable number of responses
- Fixed Interval:    Reinforcement after a set amount of time
- Variable Interval: Reinforcement after varying time intervals
*** Observational Learning - Albert Bandura
Occurs when behavior is acquired by watching & imitating others

The Bobo Doll Experiment (1961):
- Children watched an adult interact aggressively or non-aggressively with an inflatable "Bobo doll"
- Children who observed aggressive behavior were more likely to imitate that aggression, even without direct reinforcement

Findings: This suggested that learning can occur through observation alone, not just through rewards or punishments
Key Point: Behavior is shaped by our environment & social context
**** 4 Key Processes
Attention: Learner must pay attention to the model's behavior
Retention: Learner must remember the observed behavior
Reproduction: Learner must be able to replicate (physically & mentally) capable of reproducing it
Motivation: Motivated to imitate the behavior
** Shaping & Behavior Modification
- Shaping is a process in operant conditioning where successive approximations
  of a desired behavior are reinforced until the full behavior is achieved
- Behavior modification refers to the use of operant conditioning principles to change
  maladaptive or undesirable behaviors & promote desirable ones

Behavior Modification:
- Token Economies: A structured system where desired behaviors are reinforced with tokens (secondary reinforcers) that can be exchanged for rewards

- Secondary reinforcers gain their power through repeated pairing with primary reinforcers
  Secondary reinforcers have no inherent value on their own
- Over time, individuals learn that the secondary reinforcer predicts or leads to a primary reinforcer

** Cognitive Learning
Refers to the process of acquiring new knowledge & understanding through thought,
experience, & the senses not just through behavior & reinforcement

Focuses on mental processes like attention, memory, reasoning, problem-solving, & expectations

Cognitive learning emphasizes that learners are not passive responders to the environment but active processors of information.
It helped bridge the gap between strict behaviorism & modern theories that account for mental processes
*** Latent Learning - Edward Tolman
Learning that occurs without obvious reinforcement and remains hidden until there's motivation to demonstrate it

Rat Study:
- Trained rats in a maze without a reward
- Once food was introduced as a reward, the rats with prior experience
  completed the maze faster
- When a reward was introduced the rats had a mental map of the maze
This contradicted behaviorist beliefs that all learning requires reinforcement

*** Insight Learning - Wolfgang Kohler
A sudden realization or "Aha!" moment where a solution to a problem emerges without trial-&-error

Famous Study:
- Kohler observed chimpanzees solving problems, like stacking boxes or using sticks to retrieve bananas
- Rather than random trial-and-error, the chimps appeared to pause & suddenly “see” the solution

Learning can involve restructuring information in the mind rather than simple stimulus-response conditioning

** Biological Constraints
Organisms are biologically predisposed to learn certain behaviors more easily than others due to evolutionary adaptations

- Taste aversion(Garcia effect): Rats learn to avoid food associated with illness after just one trial
- Instinctive drift: Learned behaviors can be overridden by instinctual behaviors
*** Preparedness & Phobias
- Humans & animals are biologically “prepared” to learn associations that are relevant to survival
- This is believed to be due to evolutionary mechanisms that helped our ancestors avoid danger
*** Brain Structures
- Hippocampus: Crucial for forming new memories | Declaring specific memories
- Amygdala: Involved in emotional learning,especially fear conditioning.
- Cerebellum: Important for procedural learning, like motor skills & classical conditioning of reflexes
- Prefrontal Cortex: Engaged in executive functions, decision-making, & evaluating consequences in operant learning
** Cultural Influences
Observational learning is more prominent in some Indigenous & collectivist cultures:

- Children in Mayan or Inuit communities often learn by watching adults & participating in real tasks from a young age
- In contrast, Western education systems tend to emphasize formal instruction & individual performance
- Some cultures value group success; meanwhile others reward individual achievement & competition

Language & Cognitive Framing:
The language spoken within a culture can shape how we categorize & remember information

Cultural Expectations:
- Western cultures: Analytical thinking; Focus on individual elements & objects
- Eastern cultures: Holistic thinking;   Focus on relationships & context
** Applications of Learning
*** Education & the Classroom
Classroom Management:
- Positive reinforcement
- Punishment

Curriculum Design:
- Lessons are sequenced using shaping
- Active learning stratigies
*** Behavior Therapy & Mental Health
Behavioral Therapies:
- Systematic desensitization (classical conditioning)
- Token economies (operant conditioning)

Addiction Treatment:
- Aversion Therapy: Associates unwanted behaviors with unpleasant outcomes
- Contingency Management: Uses incentives to encourage sobriety

*** Parenting & Child Development
Reinforcement & Punishment

Modeling Behavior: Children imitate parents, siblings, & media role models (observational learning)
*** Animal Training
Animal Trainers rely on operant conditioning
- Clicker Training
- Shapng helps animals learn complex behaviors step-by-step

*** Workplace & Organizational
- Employee Training: New behaviors introduced through modeling & reinforcement
- Incentive Programs: Variable ratio schedules used to maintain high productivity
- Behavioral Interventions: Programs may target safety using token systems or feedback loops
*** Public Health & Safety
- Behavioral Nudges: Campaigns use classical conditioning
- Smoking Cessation Programs: Often use behavioral contracts, reinforcement systems, & social modeling to promote change
